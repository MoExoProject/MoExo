MoExo:

A Multi-Directional Force Feedback Teleoperation System for Contact-Rich Manipulation

Abstract

We introduce MoExo, a low-cost exoskeleton glove teleoperation system with multi-directional force feedback.

MoExo integrates a 12-DoF force feedback system that delivers force feedback with high directional accuracy. Through a dynamic control algorithm, we achieve real-time transmission of external forces from the follower dexterous hand to the teacher exoskeleton glove. In addition, the exoskeleton glove is designed with the same kinematic structure as the follower dexterous hand, enabling intuitive control. We validate the force feedback effectiveness of MoExo through contact-rich task experiments under visual occlusion.

Experimental results confirm the system's capability to transmit force vectors, while significantly improving manipulation success rates in contact-intensive operations.

System Design

System Layout: Left: MoExo has the same kinematic structure with the dextrous hand. Right: For hand teleoperation, the leader-follower architecture utilizes direct joint copy between the leader hand (MoExo Glove) and follower hand (dexterous hand), enabling real-time joint position mirroring. For arm teleoperation, our system tracks the operator's wrist pose using a VR controller, then computes optimal joint angles for the robotic arm through a Pinocchio-based inverse kinematics algorithm

Analysis of Results

We evaluate the effect of multi-directional force feedback by comparing teleoperation performance under three conditions: (1) lateral-direction force feedback only, (2) normal-direction force feedback only, and (3) multi-directional force feedback.

Performance Comparison: Success rates and average completion times for three force feedback modes. The multi-directional condition achieves the highest success rate and shortest completion time, confirming that multi-directional force feedback significantly enhances teleoperation performance under visual occlusion.

Imitation Learning with MoExo

Pick doll and place: We collected 30 demonstrations totaling approximately 10 minutes of data. This long-horizon task consists of multiple sequential steps: (1) locating the doll, (2) opening the hand for proper grasping, (3) transferring it to the target plate, and (4) place it on the plate.

Push cup to target: This contact-sensitive task involves pushing the lightweight cup to a designated position while preventing it from tipping over. Due to the cup's high center of mass, excessive force easily destabilizes it, requiring real-time force-feedback adjustments during data collection.
